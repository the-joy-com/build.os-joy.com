# session 15 of building The Joy in the open

## first, get the vision out of my head and into a tracker

I'm starting this session not in code but in [Linear](https://linear.app/): a project for The Joy. The reason is plain — holding the whole vision in my own head has started to cost more than it gives. `ROADMAP.md` and `TODOS.md` were supposed to be the place the big map lives so I don't have to carry it, but they've become the opposite: two long flat lists I re-read top to bottom every time I want to find the one next thing, and every read drags the entire backlog back into working memory. The agentic OS, the sovereignty stack, the integrations, the anti-pacification engine, the bootstrap TODOs — all of it surfaces at once, and the clutter is exactly the kind of digital noise The Joy itself is meant to filter. Building the thing badly in the very way the thing exists to fix.

So before any more kernel or shell work, I'm moving the vision into Linear — somewhere I can park the whole map *outside* my head and pull up only the slice I'm working on, leaving the rest collapsed and quiet until I ask for it. The markdown files stay as the open-source record of the thinking; Linear becomes the working surface that decides what's in front of me right now.

That's today's first move: zoom all the way out, get the map onto a wall I can step back from, then zoom back in to the one slice that's actually next.

## the loop: one item at a time, draft → shoot → drain

The way I worked the move matters as much as the move, because doing it badly would have re-created the exact pile I was escaping. The rule was: **one roadmap bullet at a time.** For each line I'd get a keep-or-drop read, a proposed home (which of the six projects below) and a proposed priority with a one-line justification — then *stop* and wait for an explicit "shoot" before anything was written to Linear. Only on approval did the issue get created; and the instant it did, the source line in `ROADMAP.md` was **deleted**. Items already done, or that I decided to drop, had their line drained the same way with no ticket behind them.

The draining is the part I'd defend hardest. If the roadmap and Linear both held the same item, I'd be back to two maps to reconcile — so every bullet ends up in exactly one place: live in Linear, or gone. By the end of the pass `ROADMAP.md` held nothing but its empty section headers, so I deleted the file outright — the wall is in Linear now, and a hollow markdown husk pointing nowhere is just more of the noise this whole move existed to clear. (No Linear write — create, edit, re-parent, re-prioritise — ever happened without an explicit go for that specific change. A *question* about why I'd placed something somewhere is not permission to move it.)

## the wall has six rooms, sorted by *kind of work*

Before filing anything I had to decide what the columns even are, and the trap was obvious once I named it: if you bucket by the *resource a task consumes*, every networked task lands in "drivers" and every always-on task lands in "kernel," and both become meaningless catch-alls. So the projects are sorted by the **kind of work**, not the resource:

- **kernel** — the always-on cognitive core; self-monitoring runtime; the resilience layer (durability, local fallback, availability).
- **shell** — the human interface: UI/UX, the input layer.
- **drivers** — connectors to the outside world (messaging, platform/AI APIs, devices). Connector-*building* only; capabilities built *on* a connector live elsewhere.
- **promotion** — go-to-market for The Joy itself.
- **studio** — the creative application layer: the symbiot's own content and creative output (the book lives here).
- **cyberspace ICE** — *Intrusion Countermeasures Electronics*: the application layer that **defends the human symbiot**, on two fronts. Digital (identity, reputation, footprint) and psychological (psy-ops countermeasures against pacification and manipulation — inward, my own spirals; outward, gaslighting and history-rewriting by others). The anti-pacification cognitive engine lives here, not in kernel.

`studio` and `cyberspace ICE` are the two halves of an application layer sitting *above* kernel and drivers — creative output vs. defending my digital existence. New work that's an *application for my life* belongs in one of those rather than being forced down into the core.

## what came off the UI/UX shelf

The `### UI/UX` ideas drained into **shell**: inline autocompletion in input prompts (I nearly dropped this as already-done, then caught that the *prompt-level* autocompletion wasn't built and filed it); copy-paste / drag-and-drop into the input; a kill / reboot control for the running shell; and multimodality, filed as an **umbrella** issue with a child per modality. That last one is where a pattern I leaned on all session first showed up — split a fat capability into an umbrella plus pluggable children, and keep the *capability* (what the shell can accept) separate from the *driver* (the specific connector that carries each modality).

## the anti-pacification engine: one spec block, twenty-odd rungs

The whole `### anti-pacification cognitive engine` spec — about two dozen bullets — became a single cluster under one parent issue in cyberspace ICE, cross-wired so the substrate pieces (memory, the CRM, highlights) visibly feed the higher-order ones (reality-checking, relationship-tending, the compass, tactical "what next"). A few calls inside it are worth keeping on the record:

- **The reactive/proactive split.** "What should I do next?" on demand is a different rung from the system *unprompted* surfacing the next best thing with full context in mind. Same distinction I'd honoured in the shell; same distinction here — being asked is not the same capability as choosing to speak.
- **The book belongs in studio, not here.** The "gather material for the book" bullet sat in the engine spec, but gathering creative material is *creative output*, not psychological defense — so it went to **studio**, related to the engine rather than nested under it. It's a High, because writing the book actually matters to me, not a someday-maybe.
- **Priorities skew High on purpose.** Most of the engine children are High — the crisis anchor, daily routines, time-tracking, the kakebō for day-to-day visibility on my pecuniary power, the reward-hacking guard that's a lot of what keeps me going. I deliberately set *zero* Urgents inline.

## the one thing I deliberately deferred: the urgency pass

That last point is its own decision. Urgency only means something *comparatively* — assigning "Urgent" ticket-by-ticket during intake just inflates the label until it's noise. So I held every Urgent call for a single dedicated pass *after* the roadmap is fully drained, judging the whole High-priority field at once and promoting only the genuinely highest-stakes few. The roadmap is drained now, so that pass — plus a small title-casing retrofit on the earliest tickets, and a decision on whether `TODOS.md` gets the same treatment — is what's queued next. Nothing gets promoted without the same draft-then-shoot approval every other write got.

## the urgency pass: one Urgent is the honest answer

Then I ran it. Laid the whole High-priority field out at once — a few dozen issues — and looked for the genuinely highest-stakes few, because that's the only thing the word means. What the pass surfaced is that two different kinds of "urgent" pull in opposite directions. There's *build-order* urgent — the substrate everything else stands on — and there's *human-stakes* urgent — the features whose absence is actually dangerous, not just suboptimal. And they disagree, which turned out to be the whole answer: the highest human-stakes tickets are the crisis ones — the non-pacifying anchor during a spiral, the grounding point during deep isolation — but those sit in the application layer *above* the core. Promoting them today would mean an Urgent blocked by a wall of non-Urgents, which is exactly the label inflation the pass exists to prevent. Urgent has to mean highest-stakes *and* actionable now, or it's just "important someday" wearing a hotter colour.

So at this stage Urgent collapses to one thing: secrets management — the single primitive every connector, every backup, every external call leans on, and the only thing that makes "never hard-code a credential" enforceable instead of aspirational. I promoted that one and renamed it while I was there, from "secrets manager" to "secrets management" — a thing you stand up once versus a discipline the whole system practises. The crisis anchors stay High, first in line for the *next* pass the moment the substrate they need exists. One Urgent in the whole field. That's not timidity, it's the point. I also declined to rush the cloud-LLM work — the Mistral driver stays High — but I did open a sibling for running open-source models through Scaleway, so sovereignty isn't bound to a single vendor even at the cloud tier.

## then the same broom through the TODOs

With the roadmap gone, the bootstrap TODOs were the last flat list still dragging the whole backlog into view on every read, so they got the same loop: one line at a time, draft → shoot → drain. The thing that reframed the rest fell out halfway through. The OpenClaw migration was sitting in the list looking like one chore among many — but The Joy's first implementation was an overlay riding *on* OpenClaw, and shedding that dependency is the entire reason this rebuild exists. So it isn't a chore in the pile; it's the trunk the pile hangs off. I made it the parent and folded the shell, the cognitive layer, and the anti-pacification engine underneath it. The migration *is* the build.

Under that, the cognitive layer became its own umbrella — persistence for everything the symbiot says, the machine symbiot's persona, long-term memory with the right slice loaded per answer, and the life-context ingestion that turns me from the builder into the first actual user. And the promotion items resolved into something I like more than "marketing site": build.os-joy.com, where this very repo — the build-in-the-open journal you're reading — *becomes* the site. Not a brochure laid over the work, but the work itself, with a door for contributors and a way to sponsor it. The site is the open build. When the last TODO was filed, the file was empty headers again, so it went the way of the roadmap: deleted. Both head-maps are gone now. The wall is in Linear; the journal is here; nothing lives in two places.

Two principles held the whole way through and are worth restating because they're load-bearing, not decoration: the principal is **the human symbiot**, never "the user"; and the **input layer never gates submission on identity** — auth decides what the *reply* is, never the right to speak. Both showed up as constraints on individual tickets, and both are really the same stance the shell already takes, carried into the backlog.

## is The Joy for neurotypicals too? a consequence, not a target

A question I keep circling, so I'll pin my answer down here: would The Joy be useful for neurotypical people too? Yes — but only as a side effect, and aiming for it would wreck the thing.

The honest cut is to separate the **capabilities** from the **calibration**. The capabilities generalise cleanly: long-term memory, the right context loaded per answer, "what should I do next," an anti-gaslighting record of what was actually said, a switch that notices when I've gone dark. None of that is neurodivergent-specific — anyone in burnout, grief, a manipulative relationship, or a gaslighting workplace has weeks shaped exactly like mine, and would get real value. The calibration does **not** generalise, and I refuse to sand it down so it might. The Joy's defining stance is *refusing to pacify* — no sycophancy, no performed empathy, cold objective data. That's medicine for my type and an acquired taste for a lot of people who actually want the warmth and the social lubricant. The very thing that makes it work for me is the thing a median neurotypical bounces off.

So the framing I'm keeping is sharper than "it helps ND people cope." The Joy is **ND-first not because the ND type is fragile** — that would contradict the whole reason I'm building it — **but because the ND type feels the pacification threat earliest and most acutely.** Algorithmic feeds, sycophantic models, comfort sold as a product, manipulation dressed as care — that environment pacifies *everyone*; ND wiring just trips the alarm first. The canary, not the casualty. Neurotypicals benefit too — they just arrive later, once the air has gotten bad enough that they finally feel what I already feel now.

And the trap is worth naming out loud, because it's seductive: "could neurotypicals use it?" is the on-ramp to softening The Joy into a generic productivity-slash-wellness app — which is to say, *into the exact pacifying genre it exists to stand against.* For one person building this with a single real first user — me — broadening the audience now is voice-dilution and scope-dilution wearing the costume of ambition. So the rule I'm writing down: **generality is a consequence to discover, not a target to aim at.** Build the sharpest possible tool for the ND type. If neurotypicals find it useful, that's evidence the threat was universal all along — never a reason to have built it blunter.

## the wall tilted the wrong way: priority is not the same axis as "next"

A few days after the wall went up I stepped back to look at the whole thing and something was off: nearly every High-priority issue was sitting in Todo, and Todo had grown *bigger* than the Backlog. That's backwards. A backlog is meant to be the deep reservoir and Todo the short queue you actually pull from. If the queue is longer than the reservoir, "what's next" stops meaning anything — when everything is next, nothing is.

The root cause was a rule I'd set earlier in the same move: every High is a Todo, never a Backlog item. It felt right at the time — if it matters, it's committed — but it quietly fused two things that aren't the same axis. **Priority is how much a thing matters. State is whether it's the work I pull next.** A feature can matter enormously and still not be next, because the substrate it stands on doesn't exist yet. Collapsing the two is exactly what inflated Todo into a second copy of the importance list.

So I split them back apart and re-sorted by build-order readiness. The cut: Todo is for things that are High *and* actionable now — their substrate is already there. Everything High but blocked on substrate drops to Backlog with its priority untouched, because it's coming, just not next, and demoting it would throw away real signal. Only the genuinely speculative — research spikes into platforms I haven't committed to using — actually lost priority and went to Medium.

What moved tells the story of the layering. The entire anti-pacification engine — the crisis anchor, the truth-mirror, the reality-check, the personal ledger of people and actions, the strategic compass, the kakebō, time-tracking, the grounding point during isolation — is all application layer sitting above a cognitive core that isn't built yet. High stakes every one, but blocked; to the backlog, still High. Same for the connectors that carry features that don't exist yet, the book's content engine and its raw-material gathering, and the first invite-only cohort — that last one gated twice over, on the product existing *and* on it being proven for me before anyone else. What stayed in Todo was the short list that's genuinely next: the kernel substrate, the shell's durability work, the agent's own inference brains, and the handful of connectors I live in every day — mail, calendar, the two socials I'm actually on.

The arithmetic at the end: thirty-two issues came off the queue and the wall tilted back the right way — a short Todo against a deep Backlog. The principle I'm keeping is small and load-bearing: priority and state answer different questions, and a healthy board always has more parked than queued.
